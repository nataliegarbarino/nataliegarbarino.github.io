Engineering the Cheetah

Aerodynamic fairing

The Cheetah's fairing is a low drag aerodynamic shape. It was designed by fitting airfoil sections around interference measurements of the rider and vehicle.

Custom airfoils

Four separate airfoil sections are utilized in the design, each of which was custom created to optimize for low drag based on length, velocity, wobble, and air conditions anticipated at the record location. Other sections in the fairing are interpolations of these four optimized layers.

Michael Selig, Associate Professor of Aeronautical Engineering at the University of Illinois at Urbana Champagne, who at the time was an aerodynamics grad student at Penn State, optimized the airfoil sections based on the required sizes determined by the Cheetah Team. Using custom software written for the Macintosh by James Osborn, the sections were fit around the interference data. Changes in the desired section sizes were given back to Selig for further optimization. The result of this iterative process is a very smooth low drag shape.

Building the fairing

The fairing was constructed out of carbon fiber epoxy composite material utilizing a multi-step process. Once optimized, each airfoil section was cut precisely from Styrofoam slabs. A male master plug was created by stacking up these slabs, gluing, sanding, coating, painting and buffing. Then fiberglass female molds were created from this master. And finally, extremely thin and light finished fairings (one for training and one for record attempts) were fabricated using these molds.

Powering the Cheetah

The drivetrain of the cheetah is that of a conventional bicycle since it is a very efficient way of transferring power, however an intermediate gear assembly was custom designed and built to step up the gear ratio between the front chain ring and the rear freewheel cluster. This arrangement allows the cyclist to pedal at an rpm which enables him to deliver maximum power at speed near the targeted record. The intermediate assembly accepts standard bicycle chainrings, and rotates upon a pair of lightweight aircraft bearings, about a fixed shaft mounted to the frame. At the front end of the frame, a large hollow aluminum insert includes features for mounting the steering tube for the front forks, the bottom bracket (pedal spindle), and the front fairing mount. Similarly, at the rear of the frame inserts provide dropouts (rear wheel mounting locations) and rear fairing mount locations.

Bicycle components donated by Campagnolo include a tensioned Kevlar rear disk wheel and a generous selection of alloy chainrings and freewheels.

The internal frame

The Cheetah's frame is a hollow monocoque design which is composed of carbon fiber epoxy composite material which is widely used in the Aerospace Industry due to its characteristics of high specific stiffness and strength. It has a large box cross-section which makes it very stiff and strong in a global sense despite the fact that locally, the composite is very thin. One can easily compress the frame's sides inwards 1/4 inch with hand pressure.

No stress

In order to exploit the global strength and stiffness, high localized stresses at the drive train, seat, steering axis and wheels are carefully engineered to distribute the loading gently into the large thinwall box section. The intermediate gear set at the frames center is mounted to an extremely stiff honeycomb panel concealed within its mid-section. Specially designed attachment points for the seat help transfer all of the power of the vehicleâ€™s rider to the pedals. Loading at the wheels and steering is distributed via aluminum inserts which are tapered and bonded into the thin walled structure with high strength aerospace adhesive.

Learning from the Concept Z

The U.C. Berkeley Human Powered Vehicle Team named their creation Concept Z as a symbolic moniker, implying that it would be the final perfect iteration in the design process. Though, it became clear that it could be more perfect. Drawing from their college coursework, the Cheetah team targeted several deficiencies which would spell the difference between a successful design and one capable of setting a world record.

Aerodynamics ➔ The Concept Z was designed using a published NACA airfoil profile which was stretched and expanded to fit the rider and frame. NACA shapes are designed for use on airplanes. For the Cheetah custom airfoils were optimized for the lowest drag at the record speed. A significant reduction in the drag coefficient was realized in this process.

Vibrations ➔ The Concept Z suffered from losses due to poor wheel alignment. A precision jig made to align wheel components during the composite frame construction was a critical improvement.

Strength of materials ➔ Every assembly on the Concept Z was analyzed for its strength to weight ratio and was optimized on the Cheetah. In the case of the seat, a heavy bicycle seat, composite back support and metal mounting assembly was replaced by a one-piece carbon fiber structure which was custom formed to fit the cyclist.

Dynamics ➔ The Concept Z suffered from losses due to poor wheel alignment. A precision jig made to align wheel components during the composite frame construction was a critical improvement.

Statics ➔ The Concept Z suffered power transmission loss due to frame deflection at a drivetrain attachment point. On the Cheetah this area of the frame was locally stiffened to eliminate power lost through deformation.

Cheetah secrets

The semirecumbent riding position of the cheetah was determined by rotating the position of the racing cyclist by 90 degrees, thereby allowing for maximum power to be produced by placing the driver in an efficient conventional racing posture. The rotation worked to minimize vehicle frontal area, a variable directly proportional to Aerodynamic drag.

The cheetah seat was formed by creating a casting of the rider's rear end combined with the rotated bicycle saddle. This casting was transformed into a plug and eventually a perfectly fitting light hollow seat. This produced a virtually seamless integration point joining rider to drive system.

Any openings in an aerodynamic fairing may exact a significant drag penalty. The Cheetah avoided this by sealing the front wheel opening with a flexible membrane attached to a frame that was attached to the front wheel. This arrangement allowed steering with a minimal opening.

With vibrations induced by wheel mis-alignment, road roughness, fairing attachment resonances and airflow induced vibration it can be challenging to maintain control and trajectory at speeds in excess of 60 mph. Hidden under the frame and carefully coupled to the front steering tube is a dampener to smooth out any disturbance which threatens perfect control.

Finally, during the final series of attempts the team came across an abandoned tabby kitten, a new pet that they dubbed Cheetah. He was, they felt, a Talisman which brought the last bit of magic enabling this historical achievement.

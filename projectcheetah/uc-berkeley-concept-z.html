Berkeley roots

The first prototype of the Cheetah, Concept Z, was built on the roof of Etcheverry Hall on the UC Berkeley campus.

UC Berkeley Human Powered Vehicle team

In 1986, James Osborn, Jon Garbarino, and Kevin Frantz joined the UC Berkeley Human Powered Vehicle Team. The team, a group of 10-15 undergraduate Engineers, had been participating annually in a collegiate competition sponsored by ASME (American Society of Mechanical Engineers). The objective for these young engineers was to design and construct a vehicle within the academic year and enter it into competition with other Universities somewhere on the west coast.

Typically, there were three phases of the competition. In the first phase, the engineers were required to write and submit an engineering report detailing their design, analysis and testing which supported their final product. Once at the competition, they had to deliver an oral report on their design. The second phase had the vehicle tested to determine its maximum speed relative to the competition over a designated sprint course. The final phase involved side by side racing over a closed road circuit. The overall champion was determined by combining a team’s standing over these phases.

The engineering process

While the goals of the competition were clearly defined at the beginning of each year, the path to completing them was not. During this process, there were many challenges to be overcome, aside from agreeing on a design concept they needed to secure funds for constructing the vehicle, locations to test their design and find a place to build it. All of these demands needed to be met while studying and progressing through their coursework.

Demands of the project

The first major hurdle was finding a place to work on a campus where available workspace was at a premium. The answer was a very small unused room sitting on the roof of Etcheverry Hall. The room itself was small, but the rest of the roof provided an expansive and scenic outdoor work area.

Team members found sponsors from local industry and networked with local aerospace companies to obtain construction materials, use of facilities, and additional technical expertise.

They relied on learning from their Statics, Dynamics, Aerodynamics, Mechanics of Materials, Composite Materials Analysis Courses and Computer Science to complete the design. They learned how to machine from the Etcheverry Hall Mechanicians and were able to fabricate the unique metal components their design required.

1988: Concept Z's first ASME competition

In 1988, Concept Z, Berkeley’s first semi-recumbent 2 wheeled HPV, was entered into the ASME annual competition at Sacramento State University. Their design was both innovative and overly ambitious, undergoing key steps of assembly the night before the competition.

The weekend’s events provided the initial ground for testing and significant design errors quickly became apparent. Despite its shortcomings, the vehicle provided a tantalizing glimpse into its potential in the final road race as it chased down and caught the competition's fastest designs. It was a harbinger to the other Universities that Berkeley would soon make its mark in human powered technology.

1989: An improved Concept Z

In 1989, a new look and further optimized Concept Z traveled to Cal State Northridge. It was clear from the start that Cal would be in contention as they finished first in the design portion of the competition. Going into the final road racing event, the team led the overall competition. However, several on-track mishaps resulted in a 2nd place finish.

What they won

They sacrificed their free time away from classes and worked exhaustively into the weekends and nights for a second place trophy? Team members agree that being involved with the HPV Team afforded opportunities that their classwork provided in limited ways. Being on the team allowed them to autonomously explore creative applications of their education, test their understanding of what they were learning and, most importantly, collaborate with other like minded students to accomplish a common goal.

Segway to a record

A Tour Sparks an Idea

In the Spring of 1989, with Graduation on the horizon, several members of the team were invited to tour Dexter Hysol, an aerospace adhesives company who had provided products used in Concept Z. Upon seeing the facility and products, a few team members emerged with an idea.

Spurred by the feeling that the potential of their design had not been realized, the team wrote a letter to Dexter proposing that they fully subsidize the design and construction of a vehicle for the purpose of breaking the world land speed record. The selling concept was that the vehicle, which incorporated many of Dexter Hysol’s products, would be an eye-catching advertising symbol — a symbol that could tie their products to an exciting and historic achievement. Over the summer, several members of the team again traveled to Dexter and, this time, delivered a proposal which secured facilities, materials, and compensation. The Cheetah project was born!
